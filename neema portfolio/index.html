About

I am a PhD Candidate in the Joint Social Work program at the North Carolina Agricultural and Technical State University (NC A&T) and University of North Carolina at Greensboro (UNCG). My research interests lie at the intersection of climate change and human migration, and how the resulting immigration policies & impact refugees and immigrants. My ultimate goal is to work in academia, where I can become a tenure track professor at a research based university as well as teach and mentor graduate and undergraduate students. As a first generation immigrant in the United States, I am passionate about contributing to research that addresses the mental and physical well being of immigrants and refugees. I serve as a Fellow at the Center for New North Carolinians (CNNC) - an organization in which academics,professionals, and practitioners undertake community-based research, advocacy, education,outreach, and practice related to refugees and immigrants in the state of North Carolina. I believe that community engagement and activism are central to making a difference in the community as well as my role as a future academician and researcher.

Teaching

Statement of Teaching Philosophy

Neema Olagbemiro
I have had the pleasure of teaching as a graduate student as well as an adjunct faculty member, and these experiences have instilled in me a genuine passion for teaching. The quote, “To educate as the practice of freedom is a way of teaching that anyone can learn” (hooks, 1994) has guided my beliefs and teaching philosophy. Throughout my classroom experiences, I have adopted a teaching style that inspires and empowers the student. It is vital for me to facilitate and inspire a student’s learning in such a way that their thought-processes transcend the boundary of pure knowledge acquisition and extend into the realm of intellectual curiosity. I have organized my teaching philosophy around four main principles: (1) Teaching should give students and teachers the opportunity to challenge and question boundaries; (2) Teaching should embrace multiculturalism; (3) Teaching should be engaging, stimulating, and allow for opportunities to interact; and (4) Teaching is an ongoing process that should allow for transformation. These principles shape my approach to teaching, both in a virtual and physical classroom environment.

My beliefs about teaching and learning are based on my personal experiences as an adult learner, an immigrant, and a black female. Throughout my teaching experiences, I have adopted teaching practices that I found most effective. In particular, I appreciate teaching and learning that embraces the inclusion of new ideas, possibilities, and multiculturalism.

Teaching should give students and teachers the opportunity to challenge and question boundaries. I believe that education is a powerful tool that gives both students and teachers the opportunity to free the mind and look for possibilities. In the classroom, I challenge students to think beyond traditional boundaries. During the very first lecture in any course, I typically ask each student to share with the class how they have come to believe what they believe about their world. Through this process of self identification, students come to realize and recognize the boundaries that form their belief systems. I then give them the opportunity to challenge those boundaries. Through discussions with each other, an awareness of other perspectives takes place and growth is achieved.

Teaching should embrace multiculturalism. It is important to me that my students learn to appreciate multiculturalism so that they can cherish and welcome perspectives different from the ideas with which they are already familiar. I strive to create a classroom atmosphere that welcomes and embraces different perspectives and experiences. This is important because it is only through mutual respect and acceptance that students and teachers can freely share ideas and experiences. I strive to ensure that my students feel their differences, be they racial, gender, or sexual orientation, are assets that are embraced and nurtured in my class. It is through the recognition and acceptance of our differences that fear, bias, and apprehension can be eliminated. As social workers, it is critical that my students are prepared to work with diverse populations. In my class, I aspire to utilize case studies and role plays that are inclusive of different cultural beliefs and practices.

Teaching should be engaging, stimulating and allow for opportunities to interact. It is important to me that I design class activities that allow for students to engage in critical dialogue with one another. Such dialogues enable students to share different perspectives on the subject matter as well as practice how to effectively present ideas. I believe this approach is particularly powerful as it instills in students the notion that their voices can and should be heard.

Teaching is an ongoing process that should allow for transformation. I believe that teaching is always evolving, and by default a teacher must be a life-long student. As a teacher, I may not have a response to every inquiry; however, I know where to seek the information I need. As a life-long student, I welcome my own learning, through discussions and feedback from the students, as much as they learn from me.

In summary, I desire to be a teacher that invests in my students beyond the classroom setting. I deliberately arrive ten minutes before class begins so I can have the opportunity to meet and chat with students about their work or simply about what is going on in their lives. In addition, I make sure I am accessible to my students through onsite and virtual office hours and encourage them to reach out to me when needed.

References: hooks, bell. (1994). Teaching To Transgress. Education as the Practice of Freedom. New York: Routledge.
